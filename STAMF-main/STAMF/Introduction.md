# Introduction

Underwater Salient Object Detection (USOD) aims to identify and segment the most visually distinctive objects in underwater scenes. It serves as a fundamental step for various marine applications, such as underwater robot navigation, marine biological monitoring, and underwater search and rescue. Unlike terrestrial scenes, underwater environments present unique and severe challenges, primarily characterized by **wavelength-dependent light attenuation** and **scattering**. These physical phenomena result in images with non-uniform illumination (e.g., vignetting, artificial light spots) and degraded structural details (e.g., blurred edges, low contrast), making accurate saliency detection notoriously difficult.

Deep learning-based methods, particularly Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), have dominated the USOD field in recent years. By stacking convolutional layers, CNNs excel at extracting local features such as edges and textures. However, as noted by Wang et al. (CVPR 2020), CNNs exhibit a strong bias towards **high-frequency components (HFC)**. While this property is beneficial for delineating object boundaries, standard CNNs often struggle to capture long-range dependencies and global semantic context due to their limited receptive fields. Consequently, they may fail to distinguish salient objects from cluttered backgrounds in scenes with complex global lighting variations.

To address the limitations of CNNs, Transformers have been introduced to USOD, leveraging self-attention mechanisms to model global context. However, the quadratic complexity of self-attention hinders its efficiency on high-resolution underwater images. More importantly, recent studies suggest that State Space Models (SSMs), such as **Mamba**, offer a compelling alternative. Mamba achieves linear computational complexity while maintaining the ability to model long-range dependencies. Crucially, frequency analysis reveals that Mamba behaves as a low-pass filter, exhibiting a strong preference for **low-frequency components (LFC)**, such as global structure and illumination distribution.

This observation presents a "double-edged sword" for USOD. On one hand, Mamba's low-frequency bias makes it an ideal candidate for modeling and correcting global underwater illumination degradation. On the other hand, its tendency to suppress high-frequency signals poses a risk of smoothing out critical object details, which are already faint in underwater imagery. Therefore, a naive application of Mamba to USOD may result in saliency maps with correct localization but coarse boundaries.

To resolve this dilemma, we propose a novel perspective: **Physically-Guided Frequency Decoupling**. We argue that an optimal USOD framework should synergistically leverage Mamba for global low-frequency modeling (illumination correction) and CNNs for local high-frequency refinement (structural preservation).

In this paper, we present the **Dual-Stream Illumination-Guided Mamba Network (DS-IGMNet)**. Our approach explicitly decomposes visual features into frequency-specific flows guided by physical priors. Specifically, we utilize an illumination map to isolate low-frequency regions and process them with Mamba to rectify global lighting. Simultaneously, we use a gradient map to enhance high-frequency details, which are then refined by a local CNN branch. By symmetrically integrating these two streams, DS-IGMNet effectively reconstructs features that are both globally consistent and locally sharp. Furthermore, to address the quality variation of depth maps in underwater environments, we introduce a **Light-Illumination-Guided Attention Module (LIQAM)**, which uses the learned illumination flow as a reliability gauge to robustly fuse multi-modal features.

Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
1.  We propose **DS-IGMNet**, a novel architecture that theoretically unifies CNNs and Mamba through the lens of frequency decoupling, addressing the specific challenges of underwater imaging.
2.  We design the **Symmetric Illumination-Guided Mamba (S-IGM)** module, which utilizes physical priors (illumination and gradient) to explicitly decompose features and process them with architecturally matched components (Mamba for low-frequency, CNN for high-frequency).
3.  We introduce **LIQAM**, a fusion module that leverages global illumination context to assess feature reliability, ensuring robust integration of RGB and depth information even under severe degradation.
4.  Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method achieves state-of-the-art performance on benchmark USOD datasets, validating the effectiveness of the proposed frequency-aware design.
